Interview with Dr. Jen, Assistant Professor of Neuroscience at MUSC

Interviewer (Kira): Thank you, Dr. Jen, for taking the time to speak with me today. To start, can you tell me a little about your current role at the Medical University of South Carolina (MUSC)?

Dr. Jen: Of course! Right now, I am an Assistant Professor in the Department of Neuroscience at MUSC. Unlike most traditional universities, MUSC is a standalone medical and graduate educational institution, which means we don’t have undergraduate students on campus. My role is primarily research-focused, though I do some teaching as well. My work revolves around studying neuroscience, particularly how different factors influence addiction and substance use.

Interviewer (Kira): That sounds fascinating! What was your educational journey like? How did you get to where you are today?

Dr. Jen: It was quite a long journey, but I have always enjoyed learning, so I didn’t mind all the schooling. I grew up in Kalamazoo, Michigan, and attended Western Michigan University for my undergraduate studies. At the time, there wasn’t a neuroscience major available, so I double-majored in biology and psychology. While in college, I had the opportunity to work in a research lab, which was my first exposure to the world of scientific research. I didn’t even know it was a career option before then!

After college, I moved to Washington, DC, and attended American University, where I earned both my Master’s and Doctorate in Neuroscience. My research there focused on how different parts of the brain control our responses to substances like alcohol and nicotine. One specific question I explored was why some people, known as social smokers, only smoke when they drink. Understanding the brain mechanisms behind these behaviors is crucial because excessive smoking and drinking can be harmful to one’s health.

Interviewer (Kira): That’s really interesting! So after completing your doctorate, what were your next steps in your career?

Dr. Jen: After finishing my Ph.D., I worked at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill for a few years before moving to MUSC. My research has continued to focus on understanding addiction, particularly how stress influences alcohol consumption and addiction. My team and I work on identifying potential drug treatments to help individuals control their drinking, make healthier choices, and even reverse some of the brain damage caused by excessive alcohol exposure.

Interviewer (Kira): It’s amazing how your research can have real-world applications for improving health. What initially drew you to neuroscience and addiction research?

Dr. Jen: I’ve always been fascinated by the brain—how it controls everything we do, think, and feel. When I first started in research, I became particularly interested in how substances like alcohol and nicotine affect brain function and behavior. Addiction is such a complex issue, influenced by biological, psychological, and environmental factors, and I wanted to contribute to a better understanding of it. Ultimately, the goal is to help people live healthier lives by developing treatments that can reduce substance abuse and its harmful effects.

Interviewer (Kira): That’s incredible. It sounds like a rewarding field to be in. Thank you for sharing your journey and insights with me. Would you be open to a follow-up discussion over Zoom to explore more ideas for my project?

Dr. Jen: Absolutely! I’d be happy to chat more and help in any way I can. Just let me know when you’d like to set up a time!
